A Report for Those Looking to Save by Transferring

Introduction

↥

LBCC's main administrative building. (Photo from linnbenton.edu)

In early 2023, the administration of Linn-Benton Community College announced a round of budget cuts for the end of the 2023-24 academic year[1]. The Computer Systems department was to be cut in its entirety, which means the Computer Science transfer programs, Network and Systems Administration applied programs, and the Systems Administration Certificate program would no longer be offered to new students. As reported in the local Albany Democrat-Herald, the cuts were preceded by a long-running decline in enrollment and state funding that's lasted over a decade[2]. In the most recent adopted budget, President Lisa Avery describes a set of revenue sources substantially reliant on the state's Community College Support Fund, as 'State Aid'[3]. Further, a graph of historical revenue in the document tracks a "period of divestment" in which every source of revenue has increased at sub-inflationary rates since the 2008-09 biennium. The cuts were extremely controversial and prompted impassioned letters. Among many others: the State Librarian and wrote in response to the loss of all library full-time staff[4], and concerned faculty wrote in response to the Computer System department's closure[5]. Despite this, the move is extraordinarily unlikely to be overturned.

LBCC's next cohort of would-be computer science students will then be forced to enroll simultaneously in another community college if they are to avoid the unnecessary cost of transferring to a four-year institution without any major-specific credits. Of course, these students would have only arrived at LBCC after deciding the logistical considerations (such as a lengthy commute) did not make it worthwhile to simply attend another community college instead. Because Oregon's universities have unique requirements and course equivalencies, it will be necessary for the student to verify their courses will transfer ahead of time. Oregon's community colleges offer Major Transfer Maps (MTMs), Associate of Science Oregon Transfer in Computer Science (ASOT-CS), and specific Associate of Science (AS) programs, all of which are usually tailored to one or several four-year institutions.

Methods

↥

The National Center for Education Statistics' College Navigator[7] was used to collate all 17 of Oregon's Community College. Though filtering is offered based on Program or Major, anomalies were found that made the data unreliable:

  • Umpqua Community College advertises a Computer Science program, but College Navigator does not list Umpqua as having any offerings under their "Computer and Information Sciences and Support Services" category[8].
  • Clatsop Community College does not advertise a Computer Science program, but College Navigator shows one award for "Computer Science" conferred in 2021-2022.

Therefore, the websites of every community college (less Linn-Benton) were laboriously searched for advertisement for a computer science transfer program. Immediate offerings of distance learnings advertised on the program page were recorded.

College Navigator data on the community colleges offering programs (and LBCC) was exported from the system. Address, Net-price, Setting, and whether campus housing was offered was extracted for further reference. College head-count and enrollment by subject were taken from the HECC's Community College Data Mart.[9]

Just 11 of Oregon's 17 community colleges appeared to advertise a transfer program for Computer Science.
The data extracted and tabulated is only a selected subset of NCET and the Oregon HECC's Datamart. NCET's Transfer Out and Graduation rates apply to students which began their studies in 2019, while their Net Price is the average for full time undergraduate students beginning school in 2021-2022. The HECC data is from Fall of the 2021-2022 academic year.

Only a few institutions immediately advertised online options on the program page, but it can not be assumed that those which do not explicitly mention remote learning do not offer it.

  • Southwestern Oregon CC: "...you can study full-time, part-time or online—whatever fits your life"[11].
  • Umpqua CC: "Complete the core computer science courses 100% online"[12].
  • Portland CC: "For the working student and those with a busy schedule, we offer all of our CS sequences online"[13].

Central Oregon Community College mentions "Many of the programs' stackable credentials are available fully online. These include CAD, IT, network and web development, cybersecurity, and software engineering, all taught with current tech platforms" when advertising the Computer Information Systems department, but it is unclear if any of the credentials are applicable to the Computer Science transfer degree offered[14].

Conclusions

↥

The dataset, reliability of the data and the strength of the recommendations that can be drawn from them is limited. The inconsistencies found in the NCES data create further questions of completeness of the data. In the absence of outside context from experts, it was challenging to find more relevant datasets. Further, a lot of vital information students would likely want to evaluate colleges using is not attainable from the websites of the institution alone, and substantial addition research time would have been required to request qualitative and quantitative information from each relevant community college. The quality of each institution's program is especially hard to qualify using the available statistics, and the HECC data on enrollment in CS & Engineering positions can only loosely estimate the popularity of related courses at the colleges.

Further, information about degree requirements and course articulations is available online, but extremely time-consuming and error-prone to navigate without the assistance of professional advisors, in addition to being subject to change. The portability of credits will probably be a prime consideration in students considering enrolling in another community college, but it can only be evaluated ideally by contacting both the university and partner community college in question.

Recommendations

↥

Students near LBCC looking to attend another community college in person would be best served at Chemeketa or Lane. These institutions have lengthy commutes from Albany, but they both offer Computer Science transfer programs.

Students interesting in remote learning have substantially more (and more flexible) options. Portland Community College has the highest net price of the three institutions that explicitly advertised remote learning opportunities, but students could possibly benefit from an 'economy of scale' from the large student count and relatively high enrollment in Computer Science and Engineering classes.
